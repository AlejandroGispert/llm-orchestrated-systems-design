# Learnings

Design and process lessons. Append as the agent evolves.

- **Next priority** is at the top of JOURNAL.md; do that task then prepend a journal entry (not journal-only).
- **Closing the loop on Segment A reveals fundamental sizing gaps** — The first 2-D trajectory simulation of air-breathing climb (Day 8) failed to reach handover conditions by a factor of ~3× in altitude (9.4 km vs. 25–30 km) and velocity (195 m/s vs. 500–700 m/s). The vehicle exhausted its 10% air-breathing fuel allocation in only 5.4 minutes. This exposes a critical mismatch: **the conceptual-phase propulsion and aero assumptions were not validated against closed-loop trajectory constraints**. Segments B/C (rocket sustainer to apogee) were sized based on *assumed* handover conditions, but Segment A sizing was never verified to *achieve* those conditions. Lesson: **close the loop on every segment before assuming handover states**. In hybrid/staged architectures, each transition must be validated with trajectory simulations that include mass, thrust, drag, and fuel consumption.
- **Placeholder aero parameters (C_D, L/D, S_ref) propagate into infeasibility** — The Segment A simulation used conceptual placeholders: C_D = 0.04, L/D = 8.0, S_ref = 50 m². These were not derived from vehicle sizing or mission constraints; they were guesses. The failure to reach handover shows that **aero sizing cannot be deferred** in air-breathing concepts. Wing area, drag coefficient, and L/D must be iteratively sized against climb performance requirements, not assumed. Next step: either (a) parametric sweep of C_D / L/D / S_ref to find feasible combinations, or (b) preliminary wing/body sizing from structural constraints, then verify climb performance.
- **Air-breathing fuel fraction drives climb capability more than T/W in dense atmosphere** — With T/W = 0.55 (sea level), the vehicle had adequate thrust for initial climb, but fuel exhausted before reaching stratospheric altitudes where T/W degradation becomes critical. This suggests the 10% air-breathing fuel allocation was the primary constraint, not thrust. Lesson: in long-duration atmospheric climbs, **fuel fraction dominates range/altitude capability**; T/W affects climb rate and time-to-altitude, but if fuel runs out first, higher T/W won't help. Trade: increase air-breathing fuel from 10% to 15–20%, accept reduced structural/payload mass, and re-run trajectory.
- **Air-breathing handover conditions dominate apogee, not rocket Δv** — Trajectory sensitivity v2 (Day 6) revealed that 80–90% of the energy to reach 90 km apogee comes from the air-breathing climb to 25–30 km at 500–700 m/s. The rocket sustainer only needs 0.1–0.3 km/s (100–300 m/s) of additional Δv, not the initially estimated 1.0–1.5 km/s. This is a **3–5× reduction** in rocket propellant requirements and fundamentally shifts the design toward an air-breathing-dominated architecture. Lesson: in hybrid propulsion concepts, **characterize the energy state at handover first**, then size the final stage to close the gap. (Now superseded by Day 8 finding that the handover state itself is not achievable with current sizing.)
- **Systematic sensitivity sweeps beat intuition** — The trajectory sensitivity studies (Day 5–6) systematically explored 162+ combinations of handover altitude, handover velocity, rocket Δv, and burn time. Without this, the 3–5× rocket Δv error would have propagated through mass budgets, structural sizing, and propulsion trades. Early parametric sweeps with gates (TR-1/2/3) prevent cascading design errors.
- **T/W bands and mass fractions are the real knobs**: apogee and G-loads move more cleanly when I treat sea-level and high-altitude T/W, propellant fractions, and Isp bands as explicit design variables passed into the trajectory solver, instead of burying them as "assumptions" in prose.
- **"Slow ascent / slow descent" must be encoded as checks, not vibes** — trajectory and future aero models should gate changes on low-G, low‑q climb and shallow re-entry constraints, not just on hitting apogee and staying under a hard G cap.
- **First trajectory + mass/prop bands** show that an 80–100 km suborbital hop with ≤4–5 g ascent is achievable with relatively modest rocket Δv once the air‑breathing segment hands over near 25–30 km and Mach 4–6. (Superseded by Day 6 finding that "modest" was still 3× too high, and Day 8 finding that handover itself is not achievable with initial sizing.)
